URAMAKI
One of the five standard sushi rolls, or makizushi, in traditional Japanese cuisine, is uramaki. Literally translated, the term means "inside out" roll. Due to the fact that it is differentÂ from the tradition of wrapping the rice roll from the outside, it could be referred to as a "rebel roll." Instead, the nori is wrapped around the roll's filling before rice is rolled around it.


Instead of receiving your standard roll with seaweed surrounding it on the outside, you will receive one with rice on the outside and nori within. The sushi chef may use sesame seeds, roe (fish eggs), tempura (crisp flakes), and other inventive ingredients to bind the rice.


As you can guess, creating a roll of this nature requires a great deal of accuracy and attention, especially when it comes time to cut it into the delectable pinwheel rounds that people love to dunk in soy sauce. Because of this, uramaki is one of the trickiest forms of maki to prepare.


Many chefs prefer to produce uramaki using their sushi mats inside of a plastic bag because sushi is quite sticky. This makes cleanup much simpler because the sushi rests on the plastic rather than the bamboo of the mat. In order to keep the fingers somewhat clean while making uramaki, it is also a good idea to keep a small dish of warm water nearby. The stickiness can be reduced by spreading the rice using a sushi paddle.
