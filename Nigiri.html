NIGIRI
Nigiri literally translates to "two fingers." As the name suggests, this is a little, delectable snack that consists of a thinly sliced layer of raw fish spread over a mound of sweet and sour rice vinegared.


Nigiri, one of the most well-known types of sushi, consists of an oval-shaped clump of rice with a slice of raw fish on top. However, sometimes it is also topped with cooked shrimp and cooked egg. The Japanese term nigirizushi, which translates to "hand-pressed sushi," is where the name nigiri originates. A little quantity of wasabi may be dabbed between the rice and the fish, and it's also typical for the chef to top the fish with a garnish like chopped scallions or ginger.


In addition to the primary kind of nigiri sushi mentioned above, there is also another dish known as gunkan-maki. This is a  form of nigiri known for its topping that has a soft or semi-liquid texture, such as minced fish roe or sea urchin The distinctive feature of gunkan-maki is the nori strip that is wrapped around the edge of the rice, giving it the appearance of a battleship. The top edge of the nori hugs the rice just so that it holds the topping in place.


Furthermore, nori-ribbon nigiri is a different type of nigiri in which a thin nori ribbon is used to hold the topping to the heap of rice. When the topping is too dry to effectively attach to the rice, this type of nigiri is done.


In that it involves rice, nigiri is comparable to sushi, and in that it is similar to sashimi, in that it contains raw fish. Nigiri, however, differs from sashimi in that it comprises of vinegar rice rather than raw fish. Another difference is that nigiri is not served with extra ingredients or seaweed, unlike sashimi.
