SASHIMI
Sushi is essentially vinegar rice that is mixed with a number of different ingredients, which can include either cooked or raw fish. Sashimi, which is translated as "pierced fish," is the Japanese word for raw fish.


Raw food that has been finely sliced is called sashimi. Although steak and other forms of food can also be utilized, seafood is typically used. Because they are less likely to be parasitized, saltwater fish are typically chosen over freshwater fish. Daikon is typically used in place of rice when serving sashimi.


Many ideas and suppositions abound on the origin of sashimi. Some claim that it was derived from a meal served at the Japanese court during the Heian period. Sliced raw fish and vegetables were seasoned in this meal with a vinegar known as "namasu." Some claim that it was first introduced during the Kamakura era, when fishermen offered sliced fish as a quick meal.


Raw seafood is the most common kind of sashimi. This might contain seafood like uni, sake, and maguro (tuna) as well as shellfish (sea urchin). Along with other dishes like tofu, sashimi may also be made from other meats including beef, deer, and horse. Sashimi doesn't contain rice, unlike sushi, but it can still be flavored with wasabi and soy sauce.


Sashimi is often confused with sushi, although the two are different things. Sushi is made with seasoned, vinegared rice, either shaped into mounds and topped with items such as raw or cooked seafood, or rolled in sheets of seaweed called nori around fillings of raw of cooked seafood, vegetables and other items.
